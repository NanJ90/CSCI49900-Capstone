## 3 Learning Outcomes

The Computer Science Department has defined “Program Learning Outcomes” which are
available online [^3]. As a capstone course for the major, this course targets all of these outcomes. These are:
1. Computer science students should learn the fundamental concepts and theories of the
discipline of computer science as specified by professional organizations such as the Association for Computing Machinery. They should:
	a) Demonstrate an understanding of the basic foundations and relevant applications of mathematics and statistics, particularly those branches related to computer science.
	b) Demonstrate an understanding of the overarching relationships between hardware and software, i.e. computer architectures, software systems and operating systems.
	c) Demonstrate knowledge of the theory of computation and algorithms (for example: formal languages, Turing machines, etc.)
	d) Display knowledge of at least two area disciplines within computer science (for example: artificial intelligence, computer theory, formal methods, etc.)
2. Computer science students should be adept at formulating, analyzing and solving computing problems. They should:
	a) Be proficient in writing and reading programs sufficient to implement and study algorithms.
	b) Be able to apply principles of design and analysis in creating substantive projects involving programs and algorithmic design, and have experience working in teams on projects of moderately realistic scope.
3. Computer science students should be prepared to pursue advanced studies in the field
and to assume professional responsibilities. They should:
	a) Be able to communicate technical ideas effectively, both in writing and in oral presentations.
	b) Demonstrate an understanding of the ethical concerns typically arising in the context of computing.
	c) Be competitive when applying to graduate schools should they so choose.
	d) Graduate prepared to continue to learn throughout their careers, keeping up-to-
	date in a quickly developing field. In particular, they should be able to read and assimilate scientific and technical material independently—from textbooks, journal articles, and other level-appropriate sources.

Your experience with computer science fundamentals and sub-disciplines (outcomes 1)
will be demonstrated and refined as you define a project and a high-level approach. Your ability to implement large projects in conjunction with others (outcomes 2) is fundamental to the task before you. Finally, the project you develop here will showcase your level of preparedness to advance into professional work or graduate studies (outcomes 3). 

## 4 Course Description

As the title of this course and the above learning objectives suggest, this is a project-based course. You will work in groups, of your own choosing, of **at least three students** to a **maximum five students**, to develop significant projects that involve a minimum of two programming languages and contain two major “components” (this could be platforms - e.g., a website which interacts with a hardware component, or an autonomous AI agent integrated into a user-facing application - or computational components, such as a machine learning training element which gets embedded into a fast runtime environment). For the purposes of this class, JavaScript/TypeScript, CSS and HTML constitute “one” programming language, and incorporation of SQL via a database will alone be insufficient unless you can demonstrate that you are solving a significant computational problem.

These requirements are general enough so as to accommodate the diversity of student backgrounds and skill-sets, but require that you make use of your programming knowledge,
adhere to good software engineering principles of abstraction in how you communicate across
settings (e.g. with a database or other modules), and develop a polished user-friendly product
that has clear value for you and others.
	**Additional explicit requirements:**
		- The source code for your project should be developed in a consistent and professional manner beginning after your proposal document is submitted.
		- You must employ a version control system (Git) and have your work hosted on a version-controlled code repository platform (Github, Gitlab, etc.).
		- To be absolutely clear, your final project should demonstrate, via the Git commit history, participation of all group members in the technical development of the project, working from May until project submission. 

The ideas and motivation for these projects will come from you: your interests, academic minor (if you have one), your hobbies, etc. By developing a large project as a group, you will be better prepared for the professional environment, where you will need to work well with others, adhere to good practices, maintain a timeline, and produce results. Your project will hopefully be a great showpiece for your resume!

**Working as a group is not a license to avoid responsibility.** Your group-mates will ultimately review your contribution and this will be taken into consideration in grading. The instructor will routinely check in with each group to ensure consistent progress between due dates.

## 5 Generative AI Usage Policy

Generative AI is an increasingly common fixture of everyday life and the temptation to use
it for quickly accomplishing tasks is considerable. Given that this is a programming and communication focused course, and you have the pressure of imminent graduation, this issue
is being addressed up front. CUNY’s Academic Integrity Policy (more information below) deems it a violation of academic integrity to defy instructor prohibitions on generative AI usage. **Thus, to be completely clear and avoid any misunderstanding, you are prohibited from using generative AI in any form to:**  
- Develop substantive code segments (see below).
- Prepare ANY written content (i.e. presentations and documents).

Substantive here is taken to mean substantial in an algorithmic sense, i.e. a significant part of an algorithm’s implementation. (Given the complexity of high-level languages, it is not possible to provide a strict definition of substantive in terms of code length, as a singular line alone may account for very complex logic.) Generative AI development of any incidentals not essential to this course (images, video, music, etc.) do not fall under this policy; however, please comply with any licenses, terms, requirements, etc. (more below) that regulate this usage.

### 8.2 Grade Descriptions

In accordance with the above, note that the majority of the submissions in this class are group submissions. The only exception in this regard is the peer review document (see below).

#### 8.2.1 Attendance and Participation

You **must** attend all class sessions and participate as marked on syllabus. Class is an opportunity for you to work on your projects, generate ideas, get feedback from others, as well as help others, with your own feedback. As will be reiterated below, you must attend and participate in each presentation to receive credit for that presentation.
#### 8.2.2 Project Proposal Presentation

Deliver an approximately 10 minute presentation, with slides, spelling out the ideas, “deliverables” and anticipated structure, tools and data of your project. Feedback on this
presentation from fellow students and the instructor should aid in finalizing the proposal
document. **A PDF of the slides must be submitted by the beginning of class.** This assignment will be graded for the entire group; however, to receive the grade awarded to the group you must clearly participate in the presentation. For example, if the presentation receives an A, but you did not attend and participate, you will receive no credit while your teammates will receive the A. 

#### 8.2.3 Project Proposal Document

A 3-4 page **double-spaced** paper giving specifics on what problem or need you are addressing,
what is the minimal viable product of your project, what are some extra features you would like to include if you have time, and what tools and data you expect to use. This should build upon the project proposal presentation and the feedback you received, and serve as the roadmap for your future work. Also, by this point you should have agreed upon a group and/or product name for your project. 

#### 8.2.4 Progress Presentations

Approximately 10 minute presentations, with slides, on your progress to date. **A PDF of the slides must be submitted by the beginning of class.** Again, to receive the grade awarded to these group assignments, you must attend and clearly participate. If you’re working with a language, architecture, or system that others are not using, please share information about it and your experience (challenges, aspects you enjoy, etc.).

#### 8.2.5 Student Interview 

On one of the last days of class (before the final "exam" (final presentation) - ideally 7/29 but other dates will be for spillover if time conflicts) you must have a brief (approximately 5-10 minutes) interview with the instructor to go over a selection of your code of the instructor’s choosing. This interview will not be graded but will be strongly considered for assessing your contribution to the project and if any violations of academic integrity have occurred. This, in conjunction with the peer reviews, will determine the proportion of the final application grade you receive as your individual grade.

#### 8.2.6 Final Presentation 

Approximately 10 minute presentation, with slides submitted by the start of class and **application demonstration**, of your final application. Again, to receive the grade awarded
to these group assignments, you must attend and clearly participate. 

#### 8.2.7 Final Project Paper  

A 6-8 page double-spaced paper giving specifics on what you accomplished, what you methods/tools/algorithms/etc. used to accomplish it, and what challenges you faced. The paper should be submitted by the start of class. 

#### 8.2.8 Peer Reviews

You are EACH (i.e. individually) required to submit a document containing a brief review of
each group-mate’s efforts (minimum one paragraph per group-mate). It is OK if everything
worked out well and you have nothing but praise (that’s the ideal scenario), but this is your 
chance to share with the instructor if the burden was not equally distributed. The review 
you receive from others, in conjunction with the quality of the interview, will be used in determining the proportion of the final application grade you receive. 

#### 8.2.9 Final Application

The actual final application source code and product. A final version (e.g., one branch that
won’t be altered after the start of the final “exam” time) must be made available for public
review via a version-controlled code repository platform (Github, Gitlab, etc.). Remember
this is your final product: it should be a working, useful application which includes a clear
description of its purpose and instructions for use. Again, a working and well-documented
project looks great on your resume.

While this is a group-graded component, what proportion of the grade you receive will be determined by the instructor’s discretion in light of your interview and the peer reviews
you received: i.e. you will not receive an A for this component even if the project is overall
A quality if the instructor has determined that you did not contribute proportionately to
the project.
